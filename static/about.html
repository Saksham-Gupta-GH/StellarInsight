Star Classifications

Red Dwarf (Type 0): Small, cool, and faint stars. They are the most common type of star in the Milky Way but are hard to see with the naked eye.

Brown Dwarf (Type 1): Often called "failed stars," they are substellar objects that are not massive enough to sustain nuclear fusion of ordinary hydrogen-1 in their cores.

White Dwarf (Type 2): The dense stellar core remnant left after a star like the Sun has exhausted its nuclear fuel and shed its outer layers.

Main Sequence (Type 3): Stars that are fusing hydrogen into helium in their cores. Our Sun is a classic Main Sequence star (G-type).

Super Giants (Type 4): Among the most massive and luminous stars. They are near the end of their stellar lives and may explode as supernovae.

Hyper Giants (Type 5): The most massive and luminous stars known. They are extremely rare and have short lifespans.

Input Parameters

Temperature (K): The effective surface temperature of the star in Kelvin.

Luminosity (L / Lo): The total amount of energy emitted by the star per unit time, relative to the Sun (Lo).

Radius (R / Ro): The radius of the star relative to the Sun (Ro).

Absolute Magnitude (Mv): A measure of the intrinsic brightness of the star. Lower (more negative) values mean brighter stars.

Methodology & Tools

This project uses a machine learning approach to classify stars based on physical characteristics.

  • Dataset: A NASA-inspired dataset containing 240 stellar observations.
  • Tech Stack: Python, Flask, Scikit-learn, Pandas, NumPy.
  • Models Evaluated: Logistic Regression, Random Forest Classifier, K-Nearest Neighbors.
  • Best Model: Logistic Regression was selected for its high accuracy (100% on test set) and interpretability.
  • Validation: The system implements domain-aware validation to detect inputs that are physically impossible for standard stars (e.g., potential black holes or neutron stars).
